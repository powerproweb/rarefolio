Backstory:

Alistair Valecourt was commissioned at the height of the Great Velvet Steam Expansion, a period when every noble house worth its marble staircase was trying to outdo the next with louder boilers, shinier copper trim, and increasingly dramatic dining announcements. Professor Septimus Valewright, a man who believed a proper introduction could prevent at least half the wars of polite society, designed Alistair to do what most humans could not: run a sprawling estate flawlessly while making everyone feel slightly more important than they truly were.

In such a crowd, one had to be extraordinary to stand out. Alistair was. Unlike the lesser automata of the age, Alistair was fitted with refined conversational lattices and a gold-threaded vocal resonator, allowing him to deliver announcements with the calm gravity of a cathedral bell. When one overeager inventor attempted to unveil a self-buttering toast carriage during a formal supper, it was Alistair who redirected the runaway device into the conservatory with such elegance that the guests assumed it was planned entertainment.

He came to be admired not merely for efficiency, but for presence. He embodied the new age: precision married to pageantry, invention elevated by restraint. In an era overflowing with bright minds and louder machines, Alistair Valecourt proved that true distinction did not clatter. It glided.

Edmund excelled from his first week. He reorganized three wings of the estate, corrected a decade of misfiled correspondence, and discreetly prevented a viscount from proposing marriage to the wrong sister by rerouting the evening promenade. Edmund Aurellian represents the finest ambition of the era: not invention for novelty alone, but invention disciplined by standards. He was built to preserve the grace of old greatness while ensuring it did not get trampled by progress in muddy boots.

Theodore Valemont was born from a problem familiar to the upper houses of the industrial age: too many brilliant people under one roof and not enough order to stop them setting fire to the wallpaper. Dr. Horace Bellfane, famed for combining mechanical discipline with social intelligence, built Theodore specifically for estates where invention moved at such a pace that breakfast discussions could become patent disputes before luncheon.

Theodore’s first assignment was Valemont Hall, a place famous for hosting engineers, chemists, aviators, industrial patrons, and one opera singer who insisted every corridor was acoustically beneath her. It was less a residence and more an organized eruption. Theodore stepped into this turmoil with polished boots, sapphire oculars, and an internal timing core so accurate he could coordinate a seven-course state dinner and a rooftop airship arrival without wrinkling a napkin.

What distinguished Theodore was his composure under absurd conditions. When the west atrium filled with harmless but extremely dramatic violet steam during an experimental valve test, he calmly announced to assembled guests that the house was “briefly expressing artistic temperament.” Panic was avoided, reputations were preserved, and the engineer responsible was assigned to potato-peeling duty for a fortnight.

His design emphasized intelligent hierarchy. Theodore did not simply direct staff. He optimized them. Maids finished early, footmen moved like a brass orchestra, cooks synchronized like artillery crews, and even stubborn groundskeepers grudgingly admitted the machine had style. He was particularly skilled in managing ambitious human egos, which in that era were almost as dangerous as unsecured boilers.

Theodore Valemont became known as the gentleman engine of order. In a century rushing toward the future with monocles slightly askew, he stood as proof that excellence was not about moving fastest. It was about moving with command, clarity, and just enough charm to keep everyone from mutiny.

Augustin Wrenhall endures in legend as one of the class’s gold-standard exemplars: dignified, formidable, and exquisitely prepared. In a century obsessed with standing out, he accomplished the rare feat of making excellence look ancestral.

He was commissioned for Harrowvale House, a place where politics, industry, wealth, and invention converged in dangerously elegant combinations. Secret correspondence passed through its halls. His adaptive etiquette engine allowed him to calibrate his conduct to rank, mood, and occasion within seconds. He was built for houses that intended not just to survive the age of invention, but to reign over it.
